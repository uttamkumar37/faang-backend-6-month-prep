# STAR Method for Senior Backend Interviews

## Purpose
Use STAR without sounding scripted, and add senior-level context: trade-offs, stakeholders, measurable impact, and reflection.

## Study Steps
- Draft the story in STAR format with concrete scope and metrics.
- Compress it into a 90-second answer and a 3-minute deep-dive version.
- Practice two follow-ups: what you would change and what signal proves impact.

## STAR Structure
| Part | What to include | Senior backend signal |
|---|---|---|
| Situation | Business and technical context | Scale, reliability, team constraints |
| Task | Your responsibility | Ownership beyond assigned ticket |
| Action | Decisions and execution | Trade-offs, alignment, risk control |
| Result | Outcome | Metrics, customer impact, learning |
| Reflection | What changed afterward | Process improvement and maturity |

## Answer Framework
1. State the problem in one sentence.
2. Clarify your role and decision rights.
3. Explain two or three actions, not every detail.
4. Quantify the outcome.
5. Close with what you changed in the system or team.

## Interview Questions
- What was your exact role and decision authority?
- What trade-off did you make and why?
- What would you do differently now?
- How did you measure impact?

## Common Mistakes
- Using 'we' so much that your personal contribution is unclear.
- Over-explaining technical context before the behavioral point.
- No metric, customer impact, or durable learning.

## Self-Check
- [ ] The story fits in 90 seconds without rushing.
- [ ] The result has a number, risk reduction, or user impact.
- [ ] I can answer two follow-ups without inventing details.

## Practical Example
Example: 'A payment retry bug created duplicate provider calls. I owned the fix, introduced idempotency keys and reconciliation checks, reduced duplicate attempts to near zero, and added a runbook for provider timeout incidents.'
