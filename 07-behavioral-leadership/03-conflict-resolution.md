# Conflict Resolution

## Purpose
Prepare conflict answers that show technical judgment, listening, and principled decision-making under disagreement.

## Study Steps
- Draft the story in STAR format with concrete scope and metrics.
- Compress it into a 90-second answer and a 3-minute deep-dive version.
- Practice two follow-ups: what you would change and what signal proves impact.

## Conflict Types
| Conflict | What interviewers test |
|---|---|
| Architecture disagreement | Can you reason from constraints instead of ego? |
| Priority conflict | Can you balance delivery and reliability? |
| Code review conflict | Can you raise quality without personalizing? |
| Cross-team dependency | Can you align interfaces and timelines? |

## Answer Framework
1. Describe the technical disagreement neutrally.
2. State the shared goal.
3. Bring data: load, cost, latency, reliability, migration risk.
4. Explain the decision and how dissent was handled.
5. Show the result and relationship outcome.

## Interview Questions
- What was your exact role and decision authority?
- What trade-off did you make and why?
- What would you do differently now?
- How did you measure impact?

## Common Mistakes
- Using 'we' so much that your personal contribution is unclear.
- Over-explaining technical context before the behavioral point.
- No metric, customer impact, or durable learning.

## Self-Check
- [ ] The story fits in 90 seconds without rushing.
- [ ] The result has a number, risk reduction, or user impact.
- [ ] I can answer two follow-ups without inventing details.

## Practical Example
Example: You disagreed on synchronous REST versus Kafka. You compared consistency needs, retry behavior, user-facing latency, and operational cost, then chose async for non-critical notifications with DLQ and replay.
